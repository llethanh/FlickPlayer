# Frame Carriage

**A philosophy for the Flick Player mark.**

This is not a logo competing for attention; it is a **carriage** — a quiet
vehicle that carries one idea across every pixel size, every theme, every
context where Windows asks "what is this program?". The mark earns its place
through restraint, not flourish. Every line is the residue of a decision
already debated and discarded a hundred times before; what remains is what
could not be removed.

The work belongs to the lineage of geometric monograms — Vignelli's transit
plates, Müller-Brockmann's grids, Chermayeff's masks of 1960s broadcasting
networks. It does not chase the warm illustrative trend of contemporary app
stores. It refuses gradients, refuses "depth," refuses the soft pillow of
3D bevels. It is a flat mark cut as if from a single sheet of stained
walnut, then mounted with calm precision in a frame the size of a postage
stamp. The viewer should sense, without being told, that someone obsessed
over the angle of a single edge for an entire afternoon, then accepted that
afternoon as part of the cost of doing it well.

Color is monolithic and disciplined: **a deep neutral ground** that
disappears into the dark UI of any modern operating system, and **a single
warm accent** that sits inside the mark like an ember inside a hearth — not
decorative, not gratuitous, but signal. Two values, no more. The accent is
borrowed from the application itself, because a mark that rhymes with the
software it represents reads as native rather than imposed. Anything that
could be a third color has been removed. What remains is a duet, and duets
demand virtuosity from both voices.

Form is reductive geometry. A play triangle, the universal verb of motion
imagery, lives at the heart of the mark — but it is not centered with
naive symmetry. It is **offset, leaning into its motion**, the way a
shutter blade hangs a millimeter ajar in a freshly oiled mechanism. Around
it, the silent rhythm of a film perforation strip — the sprocket teeth of
a 35mm lineage — is suggested rather than illustrated, hinted at the edges
in marks so small that someone scanning a taskbar would feel them rather
than see them. This is the subtle reference: an entire cinema engineering
tradition compressed into four notches, visible only to those who already
know what they are looking at.

The mark must hold itself together at **16 pixels** — at desktop-shortcut
size, at taskbar size, at file-explorer-detail-pane size — with the same
authority it holds at 256 pixels in an installer banner. This is the
discipline of the icon designer: you are not allowed to optimize for one
size and let the others fend for themselves. Every shape is hinted to a
pixel grid that respects 16, 32, 48, and 256 simultaneously. There are no
hairlines, no decorative serifs, no half-pixel anti-aliasing rescues.
Where a curve appears, it is the curve a CNC machine could cut out of
brass. Where a corner appears, it has a radius chosen to feel inevitable
at every magnification.

What the user is meant to feel, on an unconscious level, when they
double-click this icon: **professional quietness**. The same tonal register
as the icons of Nuke, of RV, of DaVinci's modules, of the timeline plugin
they trust most. Not the saturated maximalism of consumer software. Not
the cute friendliness of a hobby project. A small hard mark, made by
someone who has spent ten thousand hours staring at frames, for someone
else who is about to do the same. The craft must be perceptible without
ever announcing itself — a chef's plating where every micrometer was
considered, served on a white plate, with no garnish to hide behind.
